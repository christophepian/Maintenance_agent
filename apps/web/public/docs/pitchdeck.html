The Problem

The dilemma every small landlord faces
Delegate to a régie and lose 4–7% of rent. Self-manage and drown in complexity.

According to UBS (December 2025), professional property management fees in Switzerland run between 4–5% of rental income for a fully occupied building — rising to 7% in lower-rent regions. On a building with 12 apartments at CHF 1,500/mo, that is CHF 900 per month, or CHF 10,800 per year, extracted directly from yield. For a small landlord, that is not a service fee. It is a meaningful reduction in return on a long-term asset.

So more and more owners try to self-manage
The complexity hits fast — and there is no tool built for them.

UBS confirms: "en Suisse, de plus en plus de propriétaires prennent en charge la gestion de leurs immeubles locatifs" — particularly for small properties. Self-management works up to roughly 15 units. Beyond that, most owners hit their limits. But even well below that threshold, the operational burden is real: tenant changeovers, maintenance coordination, QR-invoice compliance, Nebenkosten statements, rent adjustment rules, document management. None of it is hard in isolation. Together, it consumes time that most landlords do not have — and errors carry legal consequences.

The six operational burdens — what self-managing actually means
01
QR invoices & rent tracking

Mandatory since 2022, the QR-invoice format is unfamiliar to most private landlords. Generating compliant invoices, tracking payments, following up on arrears — all done manually today via e-banking exports and spreadsheets. One missed payment, no alert.

02
Rent adjustment — legal minefield

Swiss rent law ties increases to the reference mortgage rate, inflation index, and cost increases — with specific forms, notice periods, and cantonal variations. Most small landlords either never adjust (leaving money on the table) or adjust incorrectly (creating contestable notices). Both outcomes cost real money.

03
Maintenance requests & tenant communication

Requests arrive by WhatsApp, email, or phone call. No structured intake, no priority triage, no audit trail. When something goes wrong — a boiler failure at 7pm on a Friday — the landlord has no vetted contractor list, no protocol, and full personal liability for response time.

04
Tradesperson coordination

Finding a plumber, electrician, or heating engineer on short notice, getting quotes, comparing costs, authorising work, tracking invoices — each intervention is a small project. Most landlords have no vetted contractor network, no pricing benchmarks, and no way to know if they overpaid.

05
Document & correspondence management

Lease agreements, entry/exit protocols, cantonal forms, insurance documents, maintenance records, tax-relevant receipts. Stored across email folders, a shared drive, and a desk drawer. When a tenant disputes something or the tax office requests records, finding the right document is a crisis.

06
No performance visibility

Is the property performing well? What is the actual net yield after maintenance and vacancy? How does it compare to similar buildings in the same canton? No self-managing landlord today has a clear answer to these questions. They know their gross rent. Everything else is a guess.

Why current solutions fail this segment
The régie
Costs 4–7% and delivers reactive, generic service

Designed for portfolio owners with 50+ units, not for a landlord with one building. The fee structure is the same regardless of property size, meaning small landlords subsidise the cost of managing larger portfolios. The service is operational — no data, no benchmarking, no forward view. Validated in customer discovery: frustration with régie advisory quality surfaced unprompted.

ERP software (GARAIO REM, Quorum)
Built for professionals, not private owners

Minimum deal sizes of CHF 5K+/yr, implementation projects measured in months, interfaces designed for trained régie staff. A private landlord with 3 apartments has no pathway into these tools — and even if they did, the complexity would overwhelm rather than help. These are tools that require a professional to operate.

Excel & generic tools
No compliance, no automation, no safety net

The current default for most self-managing landlords. Works until it doesn't — until a tenant disputes a rent increase calculated on the wrong base, or a Nebenkosten statement is rejected, or a QR invoice is formatted incorrectly. No alerts, no audit trail, no legal guardrails. The tool that most landlords use today has no knowledge of Swiss tenancy law.
